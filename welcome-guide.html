The questions you've been sitting on

Answered honestly, without the runaround

Am I forgetting everything because of dementia?

No. You're not. What you're experiencing is the direct effect of estrogen fluctuation on your brain, and there's a lot of solid neuroscience to back that up.

Estrogen is a neuroprotective hormone. When it fluctuates in perimenopause, your memory, cognitive clarity, and executive function fluctuate with it. That's not weakness. That's biology.

Your brain has estrogen receptors in its key memory and executive centres — the hippocampus, the prefrontal cortex. When estrogen drops, those areas lose some of their fuel. It's temporary. It's treatable. And it deserves a better answer than "you're probably just stressed."

Why am I suddenly so anxious when I never used to be?

Honestly, this one catches a lot of women completely off guard. If you've never been anxious in your life and now your nervous system is constantly buzzing — that's not a character shift. That's hormones.

Progesterone has a direct calming effect on your nervous system. As it declines, that built-in regulation starts to waver. At the same time, fluctuating estrogen affects serotonin and GABA — the neurotransmitters that keep anxiety in check. Take those offline and your body goes into alert mode.

This isn't you falling apart. It's your nervous system navigating a hormonal landscape it hasn't been through before. There's a difference.

The good news: nervous system regulation tools — breathwork, yoga nidra, targeted movement, vagus nerve work — aren't just nice-to-haves. They're medicine. You'll find all of them here.

Okay but what actually is perimenopause? When does it start?

Perimenopause is the transition phase — the years leading up to your final period, when your ovaries gradually start producing less estrogen and progesterone. Most women hit it somewhere in their early-to-mid 40s, though some start in their late 30s.

The thing that trips people up? It can last anywhere from 2 to 14 years. Menopause itself is just one day — technically the 12-month mark after your last period. Everything before that is perimenopause.

Here's what most women don't realize: your brain can start responding to shifting estrogen levels well before your periods change at all. That's why the cognitive stuff often hits first, and nobody connects the dots.

So if you're in your early 40s, cycles still regular, but feeling like something is off in your brain — trust that instinct. You're probably right.

Why won't my doctor just take this seriously?

This one makes us genuinely angry, because it happens constantly and it needs to stop.

The medical dismissal of women's cognitive complaints in their 40s and 50s is well-documented. Research consistently shows women's symptoms are more likely to be attributed to anxiety or stress than to hormones. And many healthcare providers receive as little as one hour of menopause education across their entire training.

You're not too sensitive. You're not dramatic. You've been let down by a system that wasn't built with you in mind. That's not your fault and it's not something you should just accept.

Part of what we do here is give you the language, the evidence, and the confidence to go back into that room and advocate hard for yourself. You deserve a provider who actually takes you seriously. We'll help you get there.

Can anything actually be done about the brain fog?

Yes. Genuinely, yes. Not crossword-puzzles yes. Evidence-backed, neuroscience-informed yes.

Resistance training increases BDNF — basically the brain's own growth hormone. Getting your sleep right (which is more complicated during perimenopause, but not impossible) lets your brain clear metabolic waste the way it's supposed to. Anti-inflammatory food reduces neuroinflammation. Managing your stress load reduces the cortisol that's competing with your estrogen receptors.

Knowledge is power. Actually doing something with it is where the transformation happens.
